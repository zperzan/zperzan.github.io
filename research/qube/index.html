Novel biosensors for monitoring trace contaminants

A Qube sensor system installed to monitor groundwater in Riverton, Wyoming.

Most contaminants in water (nitrate, arsenic, mercury and lead, to name a few) can only be measured by manually grabbing a water sample and analyzing it in the lab. This is a time-intensive process. A few companies offer optical sensors for in situ geochemical measurements, but those are limited to gasses like CO2 (e.g., Vaisala) and O2 (e.g., PreSens). Others use electrochemistry to quantify concentrations of many different species (e.g., Analytical Instrument Systems, Inc.), but data from those systems is hard to interpret and it can be impossible to deconvolve multiple overlapping signals.

We’re working with Quantitative BioSciences, Inc. to develop and deploy sensor systems that use customized biosensors strains to measure a suite of contaminants. Current sensing capabilities include arsenic, lead, mercury, cadmium, nitrate, nitrite, ammonium, phosphorus, and more. We’ve deployed these systems remotely (ie, fully solar-powered and autonomous) and in harsh environments (ie, subject to driving wind, snow and temperatures from -10 to 110° F).
